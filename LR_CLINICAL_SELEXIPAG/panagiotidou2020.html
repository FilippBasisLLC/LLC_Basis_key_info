ABSTRACT
Introduction: Selexipag is a first-in-class, oral, long-acting, selective, non-prostanoid agonist of the
prostacyclin receptor (IP receptor), indicated for the treatment of symptomatic adult pulmonary arterial
hypertension (PAH). It was designed with the objective to surpass the inconveniences associated with
standard prostanoid therapy, presenting fewer adverse effects and comparable hemodynamic benefits.
Areas covered: This review describes the pharmacologic properties of selexipag and presents the
clinical trials that have been completed or are currently ongoing regarding its clinical efficacy, safety,
and tolerability. The pivotal GRIPHON study is extensively presented.
Expert opinion: Selexipag is the first IP receptor to reduce the morbidity/mortality composite endpoint
of the GRIPHON study, a large, randomized, placebo-controlled study. The TRITON study failed to
demonstrate a clear benefit of initial triple oral therapy including selexipag compared to initial double
oral therapy. Current guidelines do not provide definitive recommendations regarding the place of
selexipag in the treatment algorithm of PAH. Finally, the possibility of transition between the several
drugs acting in the prostacyclin pathway, and the potential role of selexipag in chronic thromboembolic
pulmonary hypertension and pediatric PAH is currently being examined, possibly expanding its future
use.

1. Introduction
Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) represents a chronic
progressive disease of the pulmonary vasculature character-
ized by abnormal elevation of mean pulmonary arterial pres-
sure (mPAP) 20 mmHg, and pulmonary vascular resistance
(PVR) 3 Wood units, while pulmonary artery wedge pressure
(PAWP) remains normal (15 mmHg) [1,2]. PAH is a rare dis-
ease with an annual incidence of 17.6/million adult inhabi-
tants and a prevalence of 10.626/million adult inhabitants [3],
which is associated with extremely debilitating symptoms and
high mortality. If left untreated, the median survival is 1 year in
scleroderma patients [4] and 2.8 years in idiopathic PAH (IPAH)
[5]. However, median survival has significantly improved since
the development of multiple therapeutic compounds; the
French registry reports a median survival of approximately
6 years [6], the UK registry of approximately 4 years [7],
while according to the REVEAL study, median survival in
patients with IPAH is greater than 7 years [8].
During the last four decades, the therapeutic armamentar-
ium of PAH was developed focusing on the three main patho-
physiological pathways, namely the prostacyclin, the nitric
oxide and the endothelin-1 pathways [9]. Epoprostenol and
prostacyclin analogs, such as treprostinil and iloprost were the
earliest specific drugs developed targeting the prostacyclin
pathway. Historically, epoprostenol is the only drug to signifi-
cantly lengthen survival in a randomized control trial [10].
However, its short half-life demands a continuous intravenous
infusion creating significant inconveniences. Adverse effects
such as diarrhea, nausea, and flushing are common and con-
stitute a major drawback limiting its use [11].
Oral selexipag represents a first-in-class, long-acting, selec-
tive, non-prostanoid agonist of the prostacyclin receptor (IP
receptor) that is expected to surpass the inconveniences asso-
ciated with standard prostanoid therapy, offering improved
quality of life with less adverse effects and comparable hemo-
dynamic benefits [12]. It is incorporated in the European treat-
ment guidelines as an option for monotherapy or in
combination with an endothelin receptor agonist (ERA) and/
or a phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor (PDE5i) in patients with
PAH and WHO functional class (FC) II or III [1].
Moreover, recent pediatric-specific recommendations
include selexipag and other oral or inhaled drugs acting on
the prostacyclin pathway in the treatment algorithm as add-
on therapies in non-severely affected patients [13]. It needs to
be noted that the only approved therapies in pediatric PAH
are sildenafil and bosentan, but the rest specific treatments
have long been used in expert centers [14]. Pediatric use of
selexipag has been reported since 2017 [15,16]. To date, only
one prospective multicenter study evaluated the safety and
efficacy of the drug in 15 children with PAH, followed-up for
a median of 8 months [14]. Selexipag had a satisfactory safety
profile, improved hemodynamic and functional variables in
approximately 50% of participants, and stabilized an addi-
tional 25% of patients [14]. Two patients finally underwent
lung transplantation, and one transitioned to intravenous

1.1. Overview of the market
The prostacyclin pathway is a highly significant target for the
control of disease progression with intravenous (iv) epopros-
tenol and subcutaneous (sc) treprostinil being the cornerstone
of treatment of severe FC IV PAH [1]. However, patients experi-
ence difficulties in use and negative effects in their quality of
life, associated with the continuous mode of administration
[19]. Severe complications, such as infection, and rebound
pulmonary hypertension, if infusion is stopped abruptly, can
occur. Furthermore, nonspecific receptor interactions create
several adverse effects such as diarrhea, nausea, jaw pain,
and flushing [1]. An intriguing observation from the REVEAL
registry is that only 56% of patients with PAH-related death
were under intravenous prostacyclin treatment [8].
The necessity of an oral drug led to the development of the
first oral prostacyclin analog, beraprost sodium. However,
beraprost did not succeed to maintain its clinical benefits in
the long term [20]. Selexipag was discovered in 2007 and was
envisioned to provide an oral therapeutic option with suffi-
cient efficacy and improved tolerability [21,22]. The high selec-
tivity for the IP receptor and the long half-life were considered
fundamental for this purpose [23].
Oral treprostinil was designed to serve a similar rationale to
that of selexipag. The FREEDOM-M study produced positive
results for oral treprostinil and led to approval by the Food
and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of PAH
patients in WHO FC class IIIII [24]. However, improvement of
6-minute walking test (6MWT) at 12 weeks was not satisfactory
[23 m; p = 0.0125]. Recently, results from FREEDOM-EV, a dou-
ble-blind, placebo-controlled, event-driven trial of oral trepros-
tinil added to background monotherapy in PAH, were
reported. A clinical worsening event was noted in 26% of the
oral treprostinil group, compared to the 36% of the placebo
group (p = 0.028) [25]. To date, evidence remains limited
especially in the more severely affected patients. Further trials
are warranted to investigate efficacy of oral treprostinil in
comparison to selexipag and parenteral treatments.
Ralinepag is a next-generation, oral, non-prostanoid, selec-
tive IP receptor agonist which has been studied in two Phase-
I studies demonstrating a favorable safety and tolerability
profile [26]. In a recent Phase II study, ralinepag reduced PVR
compared with placebo (p = 0.02) in PAH patients on mono-
therapy (41%) or dual combination (59%) background ther-
apy [27].
Regarding the inhaled route, both inhaled treprostinil and
inhaled iloprost are incorporated in the European and
American treatment guidelines with a recommendation for
use as a second agent in FC III patients or as a third agent in
FC IV patients which refuse or present a contraindication to
parenteral treatments [1,28]. They both necessitate frequent
dosing and associate with typical prostanoid side effects and
symptoms from the upper airway [11].
1.2. Chemistry of the compound
Selexipag is a prodrug, which is hydrolyzed to an active meta-
bolite, ACT-333,679. Both selexipag and ACT-333,679 are che-
mically distinct from prostacyclin with dissimilar
pharmacology [23, Box 1].
1.2.1. Pharmacodynamics
Oral selexipag and ACT-333,679 selectively bind to the IP
receptor, with the active molecule carrying a 13-fold higher
affinity to the latter than the primary compound [23].

prodrome and 6.213.5 hours for the active molecule [32]. The
effective half-life is estimated to be 34 hours [32].
1.3. Clinical efficacy
1.3.1. Phase-I clinical trials in healthy volunteers
A placebo-controlled, multiple-dose study was conducted in
16 healthy individuals in order to define the optimal up-
titration scheme [34]. A starting dose of 400 g twice daily, up-
titrated in 200 g increments, to a maximum dose of 1600 g
twice daily was selected as the appropriate dosing strategy.
Several Phase I trials portrayed the pharmacologic profile of
selexipag (Table 1) [3442].
1.3.2. Phase-II clinical trials
The long-term effects of selexipag on hemodynamic and func-
tional parameters, as well as its adverse event profile were
studied in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled,
Phase II, proof-of-concept trial in 43 patients with PAH during
17 weeks of treatment [12]. Background therapy with an ERA
and/or PDE5i was mandatory, whereas prostanoid treatment
was an exclusion criterion. Selexipag was up-titrated as toler-
ated, with a median final dose of 600800 g b.i.d. There was
an improvement in PVR by 30.3% (p = 0.0045) at week 17,
along with a significant amelioration of cardiac index by

Selexipag was found to have no efficacy in the treatment of

1.3.3. Phase-III and -IV clinical trials
The hallmark study that evaluated the efficacy of oral selex-
ipag for the treatment of PAH is the GRIPHON trial,
a randomized, double-blind, global, multicenter, placebo-
controlled, event-driven trial with the largest population
included in the history of PAH trials (n = 1156) [46]. Patients
aged 1875 years, diagnosed with group I PAH, with a PVR of
5 WU and 6MWT of 50450 m were included. Eligible
patients could be either treatment nave or on a stable back-
ground treatment with an ERA and/or PDE-5i. After randomi-
zation, patients received treatment for 70.7 or 63.7 weeks if on
selexipag or placebo group, respectively. The composite of the
time to first event of all-cause death or a PAH-associated
complication (i.e., disease progression, PAH-related hospitali-
zation, transition to iv epoprostenol or sc treprostinil, initiation
of long-term oxygen therapy, candidacy for lung transplanta-
tion or balloon atrial septostomy) constituted the primary
endpoint. In the selexipag group, a 40% reduction of time to
first event was achieved [99% confidence interval (ci): 22 to
54%; two-sided log-rank p < 0.0001], with the effect being
driven by the reduction of disease progression and hospitali-
zation events. Prior therapy scheme [47], functional class,
origin of PAH, and demographic parameters did not influence
the treatment effect. Moreover, the primary endpoint was also
independent from the maintenance dosage, allowing the sui-
table dosage regimen to be formed on individualized
basis [46].
The composite secondary endpoint which combined PAH-
related death and hospitalization was also significantly
improved in patients treated with selexipag versus placebo:
17.8% versus 23.5% [hazard ratio (hr) = 0.7; 99% ci = 0.54-
0.91; p = 0.003]. There was no significant difference between
the selexipag and placebo groups in the all-cause death rate
(17.4% versus 18.0%, p = 0.42) or in the rate of death due to
PAH during the treatment period (12.2% versus 14.3%,
p = 0.18). The 6MWT change from baseline to week 26 was
statistically larger than placebo (+ 4 m in the selexipag arm,
9 in the placebo arm; p = 0.003; ci = 124), with the most
beneficial results in the treatment nave patients (+34 m;
p = 0.0002) [47]. At week 26, NT-proBNP levels were signifi-
cantly lower in the selexipag group than in the placebo
group (123 ng/L; 95% ci = 175 to 78). The secondary
endpoint of absence of worsening in FC was not altered by
selexipag treatment at 26 weeks (odds ratio 1.16; 99% ci
0.811.66), but FC did not change in the majority of patients.
Furthermore, overall survival was not found to be affected
by selexipag, as assessed during a median follow-up of
98.1 weeks under treatment. Approximately 14% of patients
receiving selexipag discontinued their treatment due to an
adverse event (AE), in contrast to the placebo group, in
which 7.1% prematurely ended treatment (p < 0.001). The
most frequent AE leading to discontinuation in the selexipag
group were headache (3.3%), diarrhea (2.3%), and nausea
(1.7%). On the other hand, serious AEs were not more fre-
quent in the selexipag group (43.8% versus 47.1% in the
placebo group). In addition, AEs related to PAH were less
frequent in the selexipag group (worsening of PAH: 21.9%
versus 35.7%, dyspnea: 16% versus 21%, and peripheral
edema: 13.9% versus 18%), compared to placebo [46].
Following the promising results from the GRIPHON trial,
several Phase-III trials have been designed [17,18,4853]
(Table 3). A small (n = 30) prospective, open-label trial
named TRANSIT-1 demonstrated a safe transition from inhaled
treprostinil to oral selexipag in patients with PAH on addi-
tional non-prostanoid oral treatment [50]. However, 6MWT
deteriorated by 10.25 m (IQR, 39.00 to 17.90 m), NT-proBNP
remained unchanged and WHO FC change was inconsistent
(stable; 67.6%, improved; 26.5%, worsened; 5.9%). AEs were
also reported with a higher frequency than GRIPHON, as par-
ticipants were receiving both drugs during transition and
finally reached higher doses of selexipag [50].
Recently, the TRITON trial yielded results concerning the
efficacy of initial triple (selexipag, macitentan, and tadalafil)
versus dual (macitentan and tadalafil) oral combination ther-
apy in newly diagnosed PAH patients without prior treatment
[51]. The study failed to show significant differences between
the two approaches in terms of PVR, 6MWT, FC, and NT-
proBNP. However, the risk of disease evolution was reduced
by 41% with initial triple versus initial double therapy (hazard
ratio 0.59; 95% ci 0.321.09, p = 0.087), implying a favorable
long-term outcome when selexipag is introduced initially with
the dual combination. It needs to be noted that initial triple
therapy was associated with a higher frequency of AEs [51].
Finally, results are expected from the SELECT study [18]
addressing the efficacy of selexipag in inoperable or persis-
tent/recurrent after endarterectomy chronic thromboembolic
pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) and from the Phase-IV

1.3.4. Post-marketing surveillance
Symptomatic hypotension has been reported post-marketing.
1.4. Safety and tolerability
In GRIPHON, at least one AE was reported by 98.3 and 96.9%
of the selexipag and placebo arm, respectively. The reported
AEs of selexipag (ordered by frequency of manifestation) were
headache, diarrhea, jaw pain, nausea, myalgia, vomiting, pain
in extremity, flushing, arthralgia, anemia, decreased appetite,
and rash. Hyperthyroidism was observed in 1% (p < 0.004) of
patients on selexipag, compared to none in the placebo
group, and led to ending treatment in one patient. Anemia
was rather rare in the selexipag group (8.3% versus 5.4%,
p = 0.05) and usually non-serious (99.1%). Major bleeding
events were not increased in the selexipag arm [46].
In general, the AE profile was similar to that of other drugs
acting on the prostacyclin pathway. Occurrence was more
frequent during the initial titration phase and tend to diminish
over time. Moreover, AEs appeared to be manageable in an
outpatient basis with basic pharmaceutical treatment. Finally,
the frequency of hypotension was low in patients receiving
selexipag or placebo (5 vs. 3.1% of patients), while sinus
tachycardia was seen in 11.3 and 8.8% of selexipag and pla-
cebo recipients, correspondingly [46].
Children appear to safely tolerate selexipag, according to
the literature published [1416]. Up-titration was achieved
following the scheme designed for adults in a recent multi-
center analysis [14], with nausea (n = 7 of 15) and headache
(n = 6 of 15) being the most usual AEs reported. Desaturation
due to increased intrapulmonary arteriovenous (right-to-left)
shunting has been reported by a recent case series but sub-
sided after a dose reduction of 200 mcg [53].
Patients with hepatic impairment of Child Pugh class
B necessitate adjustment to a once-daily regimen, while avoid-
ance of the drug is recommended for severe hepatic impair-
ment of Child-Pugh class C [32]. Renal Impairment does not
necessitate dose adjustment unless the estimated glomerular
filtration rate is 15 mL/min/1.73 m
2
[32].
1.5. Regulatory affairs
Selexipag (Uptravi
@
, Actelion Pharmaceuticals) was approved
by the FDA in 2015 for the treatment of WHO Group I PAH to
delay disease progression and reduce the risk of hospitaliza-
tion for PAH [54]. It was also approved in the European Union
by the European Medicines Agency with the indication of the
long-term treatment of PAH in adult patients with WHO FC II
or III as combination therapy in patients insufficiently con-
trolled with an ERA and/or a PDE5i or as monotherapy in
patients who are not candidates for these therapies [33].

receptor agonist adds to the current armamentarium fighting
PAH. The European treatment guidelines recommend its use in

1.7. Expert opinion
Selexipag is the first IP receptor to reduce the morbidity/
mortality composite endpoint of the GRIPHON study [46].
Secondary endpoint analysis demonstrated absence of change
in WHO FC and a small, statistically significant amelioration of
6MWT [46]. Currently, direct head-to-head comparisons
between selexipag and ERAs or PDE-5is are also lacking.
Therefore, no evidence exists to favor selexipag over the
other drug categories as a first-line option.
Moreover, the TRITON trial failed to show a clear benefit of
selexipag as a third agent in an upfront triple combination
approach [51]. A ceiling effect is speculated to explain these
results, with maximum improvement attained by dual treat-
ment. Hence, an initial triple combination cannot be justified,
also taking into account the increased frequency of AEs
reported with that approach. However, the exploratory analy-
sis indicated a signal for improved long-term outcomes with
initial triple versus initial double therapy [51].
Notably, a recent post-hoc analysis of the GRIPHON study
indicated that patients receiving selexipag were more likely to
ameliorate their risk assessment than patients receiving placebo,
when using both the noninvasive French approach (odds ratio
1.69, p = 0.0002), and the REVEAL 2.0 risk score (odds ratio 1.84,
p < 0.0001). he treatment effect of selexipag on morbidity/
mortality was consistent irrespective of the risk stratification [55].
Current guidelines do not provide definitive recommenda-
tions regarding the place of oral prostacyclin pathway agents
in the treatment algorithm of PAH. In fact, the paucity of
clinical evidence in this setting creates a gap in knowledge.
To date, it appears that selexipag could benefit patients with
FC II or III diseases on prior dual therapy with inadequate
control [1]. Critical prognostic factors that could guide treat-
ment escalation, apart from hemodynamics and FC, are right
ventricular dysfunction and history of recent hospitalization
for worsening PAH [56]. The threshold for escalating treatment
in patients with PAH associated with connective tissue disease
should be lower, as they present poorer prognosis [56].
Candidacy for parenteral treatment should be evaluated at
frequent follow-up visits, as it represents the standard of care
for severely affected PAH patients [1]. Selexipag cannot sub-
stitute parenteral treatment but may represent an alternative
for patients unwilling or unable to receive it.
It needs to be noted that caution should be taken when
selecting the highest tolerated dose of selexipag, in order to
avoid subtherapeutic drug levels, and frequent follow-up eva-
luation is required to ensure efficacy. Real-life data report
higher maintenance doses using a slower titration scheme
than the reported by GRIPHON, and fewer AEs [57]. The
association of tolerability and treatment response needs to
be further evaluated, as data are lacking. An open-label
study including the GRIPHON population is currently ongoing
to further assess safety and tolerability of selexipag [47].
Furthermore, the possibility of transition between the sev-
eral drugs acting in the prostacyclin pathway remains to be
explored. Currently, the TRANSIT-1 study demonstrated that
transition from inhaled treprostinil to selexipag is safe, yet
inconsistent in terms of efficacy [50]. Careful follow-up is
required to monitor the clinical indicators of PAH progression
and manage the gastrointestinal AEs, as patients are depend-
ing solely on the oral route for their treatment.
Transition from parenteral prostanoids to selexipag has
been reported in several case series, reporting a safe transi-
tion, but most of them lack long-term data [5860]. Two case
studies that involved hemodynamic follow-up 36 months
after transition demonstrated stability in clinical parameters
but decrease in CI [58,59]. Possibly, patients which are unable
to continue parenteral therapy, due to medical or social rea-
sons could be transitioned to oral treatment, if they present
a low-risk status, normal cardiac output, and right-sided filling
pressures, under strict follow-up [1,58,61]. Further studies are
warranted in the direction of long-term efficacy and transition
schemes to ensure safety during the process.
Finally, recent pediatric-specific recommendations include oral
treatments of the prostacyclin pathway in the treatment algorithm
of nonsevere pediatric PAH as the add-on agent when escalation
of dual treatment is required [13]. Surely, the oral route is even
preferable in children, in terms of quality of life. However, the use
of selexipag in pediatric PAH should be selected with caution,
ensuring strict frequent hemodynamic and functional evaluation,
since data arise from small sample studies [1316,53]. A large
Phase-III study is currently ongoing in order to determine the
pharmacokinetics and efficacy of selexipag in children [17].
