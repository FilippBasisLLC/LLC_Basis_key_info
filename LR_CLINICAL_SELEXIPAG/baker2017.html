Introduction
Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is defined as a mean pulmo-
nary arterial pressure ≥25 mm Hg during right heart catheter-
ization.
1
The most recent World Health Organization (WHO)
clinical classification divides PH into 5 groups based on etiol-
ogy.
2
WHO Group I is referred to as pulmonary arterial hyper-
tension (PAH). Patients with PAH are further classified
according to a modified WHO functional classification (FC)
system. Class I is patients with PH but without resulting limi-
tation of physical activity. Class II is patients comfortable at
rest but with slight limitation of physical activity. Class III is
patients, again comfortable at rest, but with marked limitation
of physical activity. Class IV is patients with marked symp-
toms at rest and unable to carry out any physical activity with-
out symptoms as well as those with syncope. Understanding
these FCs is key in PAH management because improvement
from FC IV/III to II has resulted in improved prognosis.
3
Advanced therapy, or PAH-specific therapy, often
referred to as pulmonary vasodilator therapy is directed at
the biochemical pathways involved in the pathogenesis of
PH, rather than the underlying cause of the PH.
4
Current
therapeutic options include prostacyclin pathway agonists
(epoprostenol, treprostinil, and iloprost), endothelin recep-
tor antagonists (ERAs; ambrisentan, bosentan, macitentan),
phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE-5) inhibitors (sildenafil,
tadalafil), and guanylate cyclase stimulant (riociguat).
5
Patients with persistent PH with WHO functional class II,
III, or IV are considered for advanced therapy after diagnos-
tic right heart catheterization with vasoreactivity testing and
extensive investigations for the etiology of PH have been

Abstract
Objective: To evaluate the data supporting the approval of selexipag and discuss its potential place in therapy for
managing pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). Data Sources: A systematic review of the literature for all relevant
articles was performed through January 16, 2017, using MEDLINE and SCOPUS. A manual search of references from
reports of clinical trials, review articles, and recent conference abstracts was performed to identify additional relevant
studies. Study Selection and Data Extraction: Eligible citations included in vitro or in vivo evaluations of selexipag,
with no restrictions on patient population or indication. Data related to the patient populations and outcomes of interest
were extracted from each citation. Data Synthesis: Single phase II and phase III trials have been published evaluating
selexipag in patients with PAH. In 43 patients, the phase II trial showed that selexipag significantly reduced pulmonary
vascular resistance by 30% versus placebo (P = 0.0045) and improved 6-minute walk distance by 24 m (P < 0.05). The
larger phase III trial enrolled 1156 patients with PAH, showing that selexipag lowered the incidence of death or PAH-
related complications by 40% versus placebo (P < 0.001). Selexipag also improved 6-minute walk distance and lowered
hospitalization risk. Common adverse events included headache, diarrhea, nausea, and jaw pain. Conclusions: The specific
role of selexipag for managing PAH patients is unclear because of its modest efficacy, lack of mortality reduction, and cost
similar to intravenous prostacyclins. Additional clinical trials exploring combination therapy as well as its role in other types
of pulmonary hypertension are needed.

Chemistry and Pharmacology
Selexipag [2-{4-[(5,6-diphenylpyrazin-2-yl)(isopropyl)
amino]butoxyl}-N-(methylsulfonyl)acetamide] is an
orally available prodrug that is rapidly hydrolyzed by
hepatic microsomes to its active metabolite, {4-[(5,6-
diphenylpyrazin-2-yl)(isopropyl)amino]butoxyl}acetic acid
(ACT-333679, previously known as MRE-269).
6,7
ACT-
333679 has been shown to be a potent and selective agonist
of the prostaglandin I
2
(PGI2), also known as the IP, recep-
tor.
7
Selexipag differs from the currently available PGI
2
ana-
logues in that it is a nonprostanoid IP agonist. The binding
affinity of ACT-333679 for the human IP receptor is also
>130 times higher than its affinity for other human pros-
tanoid receptors.
7
It has lower affinity for the prostaglandin
E receptor subtypes than other PGI
2
analogues, such as ilo-
prost and beraprost. It has been proposed that the nonpros-
tenoid structure of selexipag in concert with its receptor
specificity could diminish some of the known adverse events
of PGI
2

events.
8
The beneficial vasodilatory properties persist
despite repeated treatment, suggesting that severe desensiti-
zation of the IP receptor in rats is unlikely to occur.
9,10
Rat

endothelial dysfunction, and pulmonary arterial wall and
right ventricular hypertrophy; increase right ventricular sys-
tolic pressure; and prolong life.
11
More specifically, selexi-
pag and ACT-333679 relax the pulmonary artery under PAH
conditions in a concentration-dependent manner, a property
not replicated with treprostinil.
12
Selexipag and ACT-333679
do not appear to have any effect of cardiac repolarization,
with little prolongation of the QTc interval shown at doses of
800 and 1600 µg.
13
Pharmacokinetics
Selexipag demonstrates dose-proportional pharmacokinet-
ics with no accumulation seen following multiple doses.
14,26
It has an oral bioavailability of around 50% (range 42.6%-
57.2%), which can be delayed if given with a high-fat meal
(although the extent of absorption remains unchanged).
15,16
Following absorption, it is hydrolyzed by carboxylester-
ase-1 to its active metabolite ACT-333679. Both com-
pounds are ~99% protein bound.
17
Selexipag is also
metabolized by CYP3A4 and CYP2C8 to less active metab-
olites, whereas ACT-333679 is metabolized by UGT1A3
and UGT2B7.
26,27
Maximal plasma concentrations of selex-
ipag and ACT-333679 are reached within 2.5 and 4 hours,
respectively, whereas steady-state concentrations are
reached within 3 days.
14,15

1.4 (selexipag) and 8.7 hours (ACT-333679).

Clinical Trials
Phase I Trials
A placebo-controlled, multiple-dose, uptitration study was
conducted in 16 healthy individuals.
14
Participants in the
selexipag arm were started at a dose of 400 µg twice daily for
3 days, then increased by 200 µg twice daily every third day to
a maximum of 1800 µg twice daily. The maximum dose was
continued for 2.5 days. Of the 12 patients who received selex-
ipag, 5 (41.7%) prematurely withdrew from the study as a
result of adverse events. Of the 9 participants, 7 who reached
the 1800 µg twice-daily level reported moderate drug-related
adverse events that required treatment. Based on these results,
it was concluded that an uptitration scheme starting at 400 µg
twice daily, in 200-µg increments, to a maximum dose of
1600 µg twice daily should be the target dosing strategy mov-
ing forward and used in future clinical trials.

A total of 43 patients were enrolled and randomized, in a
3:1 ratio, to receive either selexipag or placebo. Those in

PVR from baseline to week 17. Additional hemodynamic
outcomes, 6-minute walk distance, and worsening of PAH
were also collected. Main analyses were conducted accord-
ing to the per-protocol population (all treated patients with-
out a protocol violation).

Phase III Trial
Selexipag received its regulatory approval based on the
results of the multicenter, double-blind, randomized, paral-
lel-group, placebo-controlled, event-driven phase 3
GRIPHON (Prostacyclin [PGI
2
] Receptor Agonist in
Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension) study.
23
Patients 18 to
75 years of age with either idiopathic or heritable PAH or
those with PAH associated with human immunodeficiency
virus infection, drug abuse, connective tissue disease, or
repaired congenital systemic-to-pulmonary shunts were
included. Similar to the study by Simonneau et al,
22
patients
were required to have a PVR of at least 400 dyn s cm
-5
(5
Wood units) and a 6-minute walk distance between 50 and
450 m. The mean baseline 6-minute walk distance was
353.2 ± 80.01 m at baseline. Participants were allowed to
receive concomitant ERA therapy, PDE-5 inhibitor, or both
as long as it was at a stable dose for at least 3 months prior
to enrollment. Those who had received other prostacyclin
analogues were excluded.
Eligible patients were randomized in a 1:1 ratio to
receive either dose-adjusted selexipag or a matching pla-
cebo. Selexipag was initiated at a dose of 200 µg twice daily
and was increased in 200-µg increments over a 12-week
period until intolerable adverse effects (eg, headache or jaw
pain) were experienced. The dose was then decreased in
200-µg increments until the MTD was reached. The highest
dose allowed was 1600 µg twice daily. Patients continued
therapy until they either dropped out, experienced a primary
end point event, or the study ended. The study was designed
to continue until at least 331 primary end point events had
occurred (median duration of 70.7 weeks in the selexipag
and 63.7 weeks in the placebo group). Those who prema-
turely discontinued their treatment were given the option to
continue in the study in a posttreatment observation period
until the end of the study. Participants were seen at screen-
ing, baseline, and at 8, 16, and 26 weeks, then every 6
months thereafter. Clinical and laboratory data were col-
lected at each appointment.
The primary outcome was a composite of death or PAH-
related complication and was analyzed in a time-to-event,
on-treatment fashion with data censored at the time therapy
was discontinued. This end point matches that which was
recommended by a task force in 2009.
28
PAH-related com-
plications included worsening of disease requiring hospital-
ization or disease progression, initiation of a parenteral
prostanoid agent, initiation of long-term oxygen therapy, or
requiring a lung transplant or balloon atrial septostomy.
Secondary end points included change in 6-minute walk
test from baseline to week 26, lack of WHO functional class
worsening, PAH-related death or hospitalization for wors-
ening disease, change in N-terminal probrain natriuretic
peptide (NT-proBNP) from baseline to week 26, and death
from any cause. Initial power calculations in this event-
driven study estimated that 670 patients would need to be
enrolled over a 2-year period for 202 primary events to
occur. An interim analysis showed that a higher-than-
expected number of participants were receiving background
therapy and that the event rate was lower than expected.
The required number of primary end points was then
increased to 331, increasing the estimated number of
patients to 1150.
In total, 1156 patients were enrolled from 181 centers in
39 countries. Whereas 20.4% of patients were not receiving
PAH-related therapies at baseline, 14.7% were receiving
ERA monotherapy, 32.4% were receiving PDE-5 inhibitor
monotherapy, and 32.5% were receiving ERA + PDE-5
inhibitor combination therapy. The median duration of treat-
ment was 70.7 weeks in the selexipag group and 63.7 weeks
in the placebo group. The primary end point occurred in
41.6% of the placebo group versus 27.0% of the selexipag
group, representing a 40% risk reduction (hazard ratio [HR]
= 0.60, 99% CI = 0.46 to 0.78, P < 0.001; Figure 2).

selexipag (17.8%) versus placebo (23.5%; HR = 0.70, 95%

events (87.4%) were hospitalizations. The NT-proBNP was
also significantly lowered with selexipag versus placebo at
week 26 (P < 0.001).
Special Populations
To date, little information on the safety and efficacy of
selexipag in special populations is available. The pre-
scribing information for selexipag cites animal studies
suggesting no appreciable effects on embryofetal devel-
opment or survival.
26
Selexipag or its metabolites were
also detected in the milk of rats; thus, it is recommended
to either discontinue nursing or selexipag to minimize
infant risk. No information on its use in pediatric patients
is currently available. The GRIPHON study enrolled
nearly 250 patients older than 65 years, with a small num-
ber (n = 19) being older than 75 years.
23,26
Subgroup anal-
yses showed similar efficacy with selexipag versus
placebo in patients younger than compared with older
than 65 years (P value for interaction = 0.6783). No dose
adjustments are required for selexipag in patients with
mild, moderate, or severe renal insufficiency (estimated
glomerular filtration rate > 15 mL/min/1.73 m
2
), although
experience in those receiving dialysis is not available. A
once-daily regimen of selexipag is suggested in patients
with moderate hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh class B),
whereas no dose adjustments are needed for those with
mild impairment (Child-Pugh class A).
Dosage and Administration
Based on the dosing scheme used in the GRIPHON study,
23
selexipag should be initiated at a dose of 200 µg by mouth
twice daily. The dose can be taken with food to improve
tolerability without altering selexipag or ACT-333679
exposure.
26
The dose can be increased in increments of 200
µg at weekly intervals until the MTD has been reached.
The maximum recommended daily dose is 1600 µg twice
daily. Selexipag is available in the following tablet
strengths: 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000, 1200, 1400, and 1600
µg.
26
Bioequivalence between taking eight 200-µg tablets
or a single 1600-µg tablet has been demonstrated in healthy
volunteers.
25
The tablets should not be split, crushed, or chewed
because they are film-coated. This could complicate drug
administration in patients requiring feeding tubes or
mechanical ventilation. Before initiating selexipag, a patient
enrollment form must be completed and sent to the manu-
facturer. Patients will pick up their selexipag from a spe-
cialty pharmacy, similar to other prostacyclins and ERAs.
The wholesale acquisition cost of selexipag ranges from
$130.68 to $290.40 per tablet, depending on the strength.
This could result in costs of $160 000 to $170 000 per year.
This is to be compared with annual costs of ~$30 000 to
$40 000 for bosentan or ambrisentan, $8000 to $9000 for
tadalafil, or ~$60 000 to 70 000 for inhaled iloprost.
Subcutaneous treprostinil is also ~$100 000 per year. A
cost-effectiveness analysis for selexipag is not currently
available, but given the high price compared with other
available therapies, it would be of great interest to PAH
practitioners.

Summary
Current guidelines recommend prostacyclin pathway ago-
nists for patients who are WHO FC IV/III with rapid pro-
gression or other markers of poor clinical prognosis.
29
Of
these, continuous IV epoprostenol is recommended as first-
line therapy in PAH patients with WHO FC IV symptoms.
29
Other prostacyclin agonists have been shown to improve
hemodynamic parameters, symptoms, and functional capac-
ity in patients with WHO FC III.
30-33
Other oral agents, such
as the ERAs and PDE-5 inhibitors are recommended in
WHO FC II or III patients.
5
The guidelines similarly recom-
mend selexipag for patients with WHO FC II or III PAH.
5
A
vast majority of the patients included in GRIPHON were
either FC II (45.8%) or III (52.5%) at baseline.
Although the primary composite end point of death or a
complication related to PAH was significantly lowered with
selexipag compared with placebo (HR = 0.60; P < 0.001), the
difference appears to be driven primarily by lower rates of
disease progression and hospitalization for worsening of
PAH. Disease progression was defined as a decrease from
baseline of at least 15% in the 6-minute walk distance accom-
panied by a worsening of WHO FC or the need for additional
PAH treatment (for those in WHO FC III/IV). The difference
in 6-minute walk distance between groups was 12 m (99% CI
= 1 to 24; P = 0.003), representing a 3% improvement from
baseline. Consensus groups have reported
3,34
that the esti-
mated minimally important distance for 6-minute walk dis-
tance is approximately 33 m; the improvement seen in
GRIPHON with selexipag is well below this threshold. Also,
the 2015 European Society of Cardiology and European
Respiratory Society guidelines for PAH
5
recommend a treat-
ment goal of >440 m; this means that the average participant
in GRIPHON would be unlikely to reach this goal given the
therapies administered.
These efficacy findings, in concert with the reasonable
safety profile of selexipag, must be taken into context with
its high cost. Current market estimates suggest that annual
costs of selexipag are appreciably higher than that of other
orally available PAH therapies and are similar to the paren-
teral prostacyclin agonists. A cost-effectiveness analysis
would be beneficial to patients, clinicians, and payers. If its
use is either not met by the improvements in 6-minute walk

also be considered.
There is ongoing research with selexipag, including the
Efficacy and Safety of Initial Triple Dual Oral Combination
Therapy in Patients with Newly Diagnosed Pulmonary
Arterial Hypertension (TRITON) trial (NCT02558231).
Additional trials are also needed to assess its use in patients
with WHO FC IV and other PH groups. Similarly, data on
the impact of selexipag on PH-related quality of life is
only available in the Food and Drug Administration sub-
mission information, where they state that no differences
versus placebo were seen
35
; however, specific details are
not provided. This aspect of PH care also requires addi-
tional study. Until this information becomes available, the
specific role of selexipag in managing PAH patients is
unclear.
