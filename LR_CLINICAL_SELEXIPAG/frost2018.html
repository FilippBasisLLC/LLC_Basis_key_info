BACKGROUND: A long-term trial showed that the oral prostacyclin (PGl
2
) receptor (IP) agonist, selexi-
pag, delayed disease progression in patients with pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). Transition to
selexipag in patients treated with more burdensome inhaled therapies that target the prostacyclin path-
way may be considered by patients and physicians. The Phase 3b, prospective, open-label TRANSIT-1
(Tolerability and Safety of the Transition From Inhaled Treprostinil to Oral Selexipag in Patients With
Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension) study evaluated the safety and tolerability of transition from inhaled
treprostinil to oral selexipag.
METHODS: Patients receiving non-prostanoid oral PAH therapy and inhaled treprostinil at stable doses,
in World Health Organization Functional Class II/III, with 6-minute walk distance 300 meters were
enrolled. The 16-week main treatment period included downtitration of inhaled treprostinil over
8 weeks and parallel uptitration of selexipag over 12 weeks. Sustained treatment transition at Week 16
was dened as (1) receiving selexipag at Week 16; (2) no selexipag interruption(s) totaling 8 days;
and (3) no inhaled treprostinil or other prostanoids after Week 8. Clinical parameters and patient-
reported treatment satisfaction outcomes were assessed at Week 16.

RESULTS: All 34 enrolled patients completed the study. At Week 16, 32 patients (94.1%) had stopped
inhaled treprostinil and were receiving selexipag. Twenty-eight patients (82.4%) met all criteria for
sustained treatment transition. During the study, 3 patients discontinued selexipag due to adverse
events. Overall, most adverse events were typical of prostanoid therapies and started during the uptitra-
tion phase. In general, patients remained clinically stable throughout treatment and reported improved
convenience.
CONCLUSIONS: Transition to oral selexipag from inhaled treprostinil in PAH patients was successful
and well tolerated in most patients, and associated with greater convenience.

The completion of the GRIPHON (Prostacyclin [PGI2]
Receptor Agonist In Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension)
study heralded the entr
ee of a new non-prostacyclin drug
targeting the prostacyclin pathway into the therapeutic
arena for pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH). Drugs
targeting the prostacyclin pathway have always been an
integral part of PAH management.
1
Prostacyclins and pros-
tacyclin analogs are available in intravenous, subcutaneous,
inhaled, and oral forms and are recommended in the United
States and European guidelines for the management of
PAH based on varying levels of evidence.
24
The intrave-
nous forms are gener ally considered to be the most potent
and effective therapies for PAH.
In the event-driven GRIPHON trial, the oral prostacyclin
(PGl
2
) receptor (IP) agonist, selexipag, reduc ed the risk of
the primary composite outcome of morbidity/mortality
compared with placebo in patients with PAH.
5
Based on
these long-term data, selexipag was subsequently approved
as a twice-daily oral treatment for PAH to delay disease
progression and reduce the risk of hospitalization due
to PAH.
PAH patients generally are living longer, and PAH has
become a chronic disease.
6,7
Hence, quality of life, in addi-
tion to therapy risk-to-benet ratio, is increasingly consid-
ered in treatment decisions.
Inhaled treprostinil was approved after the 12-week,
placebo-controlled, randomized TRIUMPH-1 (Treprosti-
nil Sodium Inhalation Used in the Management of Pul-
monary Arterial Hypertension) study in PAH patients
already receiving bosentan or sildenal. The addition of
inhaled treprosti ni l improved 6-minute walk distance
(6MWD) and was safe and well tolerated.
8
Treatment
with inhaled treprostinil, however, is cumbersome,
requiring 4 inhalations daily, with each inhalation last-
ingbetween2and3minutes.
911
The device is bulky,
requires daily m aintenance, and the drugs stability may
be affected at temperatures higher than 30C, which is
particularly burdensome in th e southern states of United
States and while traveling.
Recognizing that physicians and patients might contem-
plate switching therapy from inhaled treprostinil to oral
selexipag, this open-label, non-randomized, single-group
Phase 3b study, TRANSIT-1 (Tolerability and Safety of
the Transition From Inhaled Treprostinil to Oral Selexipag
in Patients With Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension,
NCT02471183), was undertaken to determine whether such
therapy transition could be reasonably and safely accom-
plished; what percentage of patients might be successfully
transitioned; how to undertake such a transition; and to
evaluate the frequency and severity of any adverse events
(AEs) during and after the transition and assess the stability
of some clinical parameters. This Phase 3b study was nei-
ther intended nor powered to demonstrate superiority. It
was designed to address the questions outlined above and
to potentially provide a template for cautious, carefully
supervised therapy transition within the prostacyclin path-
way to contribute to quality of life.
Methods
Study design
TRANSIT-1 was a prospective, multicenter, open-label, single-
group, Phase 3b study. The main objective was to assess the safety
and tolerability of transition from inhaled treprostinil to oral selex-
ipag in PAH patients. The study enrolled patients in 12 centers in
the United States.
The study consisted of a screening p eriod (up to 30 days), fol-
lowed by a main treatment period (16 weeks), after which
patients could enter the extended treatment period until selexipag
became commerci ally available (Figure 1). Patients completed
the study after completing the safety follow-up (30 days) that
started af ter a patients last dose of study-provided drug (patients
transitioned to commercial drug once it was approved and avail-
able). During the rst 12 weeks of the main treatment period
(transition phase), enrolled patients transitioned from inhaled tre-
prostinil to selexipag by p arallel uptitration of selexipag and
downtitration of inhaled treprostinil. The latter started no earlier
than 1 day after initiation of selexipag and was completed by the
endofWeek8.
A guide with a suggested downtitration scheme involving pro-
gressive reduction of the number of inhalations per treatment ses-
sion was provided (Supplementary Table 1, available online at
www.jhltonline.org). However, downtitration was implemented at
the discretion of the investigator, reecting factors such as individ-
ual variations in treprostinil dose and side effects (from both pros-
tacyclin-pathway targeting therapies). The duration of treatment
of inhaled treprostinil in the study was variable among patients
and is summarized in Supplementary Table 2 (online).

For the uptitration of selexipag, selexipag was started at
200 mg twice daily and increased weekly by increments of 200 mg
twice daily up to a maximum dose of 1,600 mg twice daily or until
unmanageable side effects typical of prostacyclin therapies devel-
oped, according to the titration scheme in GRIPHON.
5
When
unmanageable side effects developed, inhaled treprostinil was
downtitrated more quickly or discontinued before reducing the
selexipag dose to the previous tolerated dose. The highest toler-
ated dose (HTD) of selexipag was the dose received by the patient
at the end of Week 12. The HTD remained unchanged (if toler-
ated) until Week 16, when clinical end points were assessed.
The study was conducted in accordance with the amended
Declaration of Helsinki, and the protocol was approved by local
Institutional Review Boards or independent Ethics Committees
(Supplementary Table 3, online). Written informed consent was
obtained from all patients.
Outcome measures
A sustained transition was dened in the statistical analysis plan
using the following criteria: (1) receiving selexipag at Week 16,
(2) no selexipag treatment interruptions totaling 8 days before
Week 16, and (3) absence of inhaled treprostinil or any prostacy-
clin/prostacyclin analogs between Weeks 9 and 16. AEs were
monitored throughout the study and safety follow-up to assess the
safety and tolerability of the transition.
To evaluate whether patients remained clinically stable dur-
ing and after the transition, World Health Organization Func-
tional Class (WHO FC), 6MWD and N-terminal pro-brain
natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP) w ere assessed at baseline and
Week 16. The clinical outcome measures were the proportion of
patients with a change in WHO FC, the absolute change in
6MWD from baseline, and the proportion of patients with an
increased (> 8%), maintained ( 8%), and decreased (> 8%)
6MWD from baseline. The 8% cutoff was chosen to account for
the variability of the 6-minute walk test.
12
The change in NT-
proBNP from baseline to Weeks 4, 8, and 16 was assessed based
on measurements from the central laboratory. Because the ratio-
nale for switching therapy from inhaled treprostinil to selexipag
was improved convenience, patient-reported outcomes included
change from baseline to Week 16 in the domain scores of t he
Treatment Satisfaction Questionnaire for Medication Version II
(TSQM-II).
13
Patient population
The study enrolled adult PAH patients previously diagnosed
by right heart catheterization (mean pulmonary arterial pressure
25 mm Hg with pulmonary artery wedge pressure or left ven-
tricular end-diastolic pressure 15 mm Hg) in WHO FC II or III,
who were considered stable. Stability was dened as: 6MWD
300 meters; no worsening of WHO FC within 30 days and no hos-
pitalization for any cause within 90 days before selexipag initia-
tion; receiving background therapy with an endothelin receptor
antagonist (ERA), a phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitor (PDE-5i),
a soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC) stimulator, or a combination of
an ERA and a PDE-5i or an ERA and a sGC stimulator for
90 days and at stable doses for 30 days before selexipag initia-
tion; receiving inhaled treprostinil for 90 days with a stable
dose for 30 days. Patients were ineligible if they received treat-
ment with any other prostacyclin/prostacyclin analogs within
90 days before selexipag initiation or any previous selexipag
treatment.

Statistical analysis
Because all analyses were descriptive and no statistical hypothesis
was tested, the sample size was based on the number of patients
considered sufcient to estimate, with an acceptable level of preci-
sion, the proportion of patients who had a sustained treatment tran-
sition. The proportion of patients with a sustained treatment
transition was summarized using frequency counts. The individual
HTDs of selexipag were categorized using previously dened
dose groups
5
: low (200 or 400 mg twice daily), medium (600, 800,
or 1,000 mg twice daily), and high (1,200, 1,400, or 1,600 mg
twice daily).
Clinical end points were analyzed descriptively. Change in
WHO FC was reported as percentages of patients with improved,
unchanged, and worsened WHO FC. Median change in 6MWD
with interquartile range (IQR), and the percentage of patients with
increased (> 8%), maintained ( 8%) or decreased (> 8%)
6MWD are presented. Change from baseline in NT-proBNP was
analyzed using the geometric mean of the Week 16-to-baseline
ratio and geometric coefcient of variation. Median and IQR
changes in TSQM-II domain scores are shown. Missing post-base-
line data for WHO FC, 6MWD, NT-proBNP, and TSQM-II were
imputed using last observation carried forward. All analyses were
conducted on all patients who received 1 selexipag dose.
Results
Patient disposition and baseline characteristics
Of the 37 screened patients, 34 were enrolled. Three
patients discontinued selexipag but completed the safety
follow-up (Figure 2). Patients were mostly female (82.4%)
and in WHO FC II at baseline (73.5%; Table 1). Most
patients (67.6%) were receiving an ERA and a PDE-5i or
sGC-stimulator in addition to inhaled treprostinil. Patients
had been receiving inhaled treprostinil for a mean of 3.3
(standard deviation, 2.52) years before the stud y. Twelve
patients (35.3%) were receiving > 9 breaths per session,
thus a dose higher than the target maintenance dose of
54 mg 4 times daily.
Treatment transition and dosing
Of the 34 enrolled patients, 32 (94.1%) had stopped inhaled
treprostinil and were receiving selexipag at Week 16.
Twenty-eight patients (82.4%) met all 3 pre-dened criteria
for sustained transition at Week 16. Six patients did not
meet all criteria, including 2 patients who discontinued
selexipag before Week 16, 1 patient whose selexipag treat-
ment interruptions exceeded the 8-day limit (due to diarrhea
and jaw pain), and 3 patients who discontinued inhaled tre-
prostinil 1, 3, and 48 days after the 8 week time point.
For most patients, the downtitration of inhaled treprostinil
was performed over a median of 6.2 weeks (range, 1.014.9
weeks) in a stepwise manner. Six patients downtitrated within
4 weeks, including 3 who downtitrated within 8 days.
The median duration of exposure to selexipag was 20.7
weeks (range, 8.1 35.1 weeks). The uptitration of selexi-
pag was performed in keeping with the protocol for all but
1 patient, who had a dose interruption of 7 days.
Among patients who were receiving selexipag at Week
16, the median HTD of selexipag was 1,200 mg twice daily
(range, 4001,600 mg twice daily; Table 2). Six patients
had dose adjustments between Weeks 12 and 16: 4 had
dose reductions due to AEs, 1 had a dose increase, and
treatment was interrupted in another. The HTD of selexipag
did not appear to be associated with the dose of inhaled tre-
prostinil at baseline (Supplementary Figure 1, online).
Safety and tolerability
One or more AEs occurred in 33 patients (97.1%) during
the study (Ta ble 3). Most AEs started during the transition
phase. The most frequent AEs were headache, diarrhea, and
jaw pain, which are typical of therapies that target the pros-
tacyclin pathway. The severity was mild for 5.9%, moder-
ate for 70.6%, and severe for 20.6% of patients.
Two patients experienced serious AEs. A 55-year old
woman with PAH associated with limited cutaneous systemic
sclerosis suffered muscle hemorrhage ; she was receiving

Discussion
In this study, we show successful transition from inhaled tre-
prostinil to oral selexipag in clinically stable PAH patients.
Overall, 32 of the 34 enrolled patients continued to receive
selexipag 8 weeks after discontinuation of inhaled treprosti-
nil, and 28 patients met all predened criteria for sustained tran-
sition. After the end of study treatment, 31 patients continued
with commercial selexipag. Careful patient selection and
assessment during and after the transition period is required to
prevent disease progression and to monitor for adverse effects.
Because all patients in this study were uptitrated either
to the highest approved dose or to drug intolerance, the fre-
quency of the AEs was high. Most AEs were typical of ther-
apies targeting the prostacyclin pathway and started during
selexipag uptitration. The proportion of patients with AEs
during uptitration was higher in TRANSIT-1 than in GRI-
PHON.

important to note that most of the patients were on triple PAH
therapy and a high dose of inhaled treprostinil at baseline.
Although some patients experienced a decline in indicators of
clinical stability (WHO FC, 6MWD, or NT-proBNP), most
patients remained stable and, therefore, transitioned successfully.
Nine patients experienced a decrease of > 8% from baseline in
6MWD at Week 16. Two patients experienced deterioration in
their WHO FC, but no patients worsened to WHO FC IV. The
cause for the clinical deterioration in the patient who initiated
intravenous prostacyclin was likely multifactor ial and may
include a progressive worsening of PAH as well as the sudden
withdrawal of all PAH medications due to inability to tolerate
oral medications. This emphasizes the need for careful and con-
tinuous expert monitoring of patients transitioning from one
therapy to another and underscores the potential e ffect of gastro-
intestinal issues when patients are relying on oral therapies only
for control of PAH. A recent systematic review of reported
PAH therapy transitions since 2002 concluded that in carefully
chosen patients, the success of such transitions ranged from
57% to 100%.
14
Of note, the succe ss of transition was mea sured
using different denitions and over variable durations. This vari-
able success emphasizes the importance of undertaking such
transitions in expert centers.
Transition from inhaled treprostinil to oral selexipag in
the TRANSIT-1 study was generally successful, safe,
and comparable to these reports. Importantly, patients
found that selexipag simplied their treatment regimen
compared with inha led treprostinil, as indicated by the
increase in TSQM-II scores for convenience. Altogether,
these results suggest that transitioning from inhaled trepros-
tinil to selexipag maintained the short-term clinical condi-
tion of a patient while offering improved convenience.
Selexipag therapy might also provide these patients the
long-term benets demonstrated in GRIPHON.
5
TRANSIT-1 provides the rst clinical study data demon-
strating that transitioning from inhaled treprostinil to selexipag
can be accomplished safely in clinically stable patients. To our
knowledge, only 1 case report has described such a transition
in a single patient. For this patient, the downtitration of
inhaled treprostinil and uptitration of selexipag took place in
parallel over 8 weeks. The patient tolerated the transition well
and remained in WHO FC II, with normal NT-proBNP levels
and normal right ventricle systolic pressure at follow-up.
15
Limitations of this study are that it enrolled a small num-
ber of patients and that follow-up time after transi tion was
short. In addition, the study was uncontrolled and not
intended or powered to demonstrate superiority or non-infe-
riority over inhaled treprostinil. Furthermore, hemody-
namic changes were not assessed.
In conclusion, we show sustained transition from inhaled
treprostinil to oral selexipag in carefully selected, clinically
stable PAH patients. Most transitioned patients remained
stable during the follow-up period based on clinical meas-
ures. Selexipag treatment was associa ted with greater
patient-rated convenience than inhaled treprostinil.
