Abstract
Purpose In vitro data showed that selexipag and its active
metabolite (ACT-333679) have an inductive e ffect on
CYP3A4, CYP2B6, and CYP2C9 at concentrations approxi-
mately 100-fold higher than the maximum plasma concentra-
tion (C
max
) measured under steady-state conditions. In order
to confirm in vivo the lack of induction at the enterocyte level,
we assessed the effect of selexipag on midazolam, a substrate
of hepatic and intestinal CYP3A4.
Methods This study was conducted according to an open-la-
bel, randomized, two-way crossover design. A total of 20
subjects received a single oral dose of 7.5 mg midazolam
alone (treatment A) or on top of steady-state selexipag (treat-
ment B). Selexipag was administered twice daily using an up-
titration scheme consisting of three steps: 400, 600, 1000, and
1600 μg with increments every fourth day. A 24-h pharmaco-
kinetic profile was performed following midazolam adminis-
tration, and bioequivalence criteria were investigated on an
exploratory basis.
Results The C
max
of midazolam and 1-hydroxymidazolam
was decreased by approximately 20 and 14%, respectively,
following treatment B compared to A. The time to reach
C
max
for midazolam and 1-hydroxymidazolam was similar
between treatments. The terminal half-life was reduced in
treatment B compared to A for both midazolam (16%) and
1-hydroxymidazolam (20%). Exposure (area under the curve)
to midazolam and 1-hydroxymidazolam was similar between
treatments, and the 90% confidence intervals of geometric
mean ratios were with in the bioequivalence interval.
T reatment with midazolam, selexipag, and the combination
was safe and well tolerated.
Conclusion Exposure to midazolam and 1-hydroxymidazolam
was not affe cted by treatment with selexipag.

Introduction
Selexipag (Uptravi®) is the first orally active, selective, long-
acting, nonprostanoid prostacyclin (IP) receptor agonist ap-
proved for the treatment of pulmonary arterial hypertension
(PAH) [1–4].
Selexipag is rapidly hydrolyzed by carboxylesterases to
yield its active metabolite, ACT-333679 [1, 4] . Selexipag
and ACT-333679 are metabolized by cytochrome P450
(CYP) 2C8 and CYP3A4 enzymes, and ACT-333679 is also
metabolized by UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT)1A3 and
UGT2B7 [5]. Selexipag and ACT-333679 bind selectively
and with high affinity to the IP receptor. In vitro data revealed
that ACT-333679 is approximately 37-fold more potent than
selexipag in activating the human IP receptor and is present at
3- to 4-fold higher levels than the parent drug at steady-state in
humans after oral selexipag administration [6]. Selexipag and
ACT-333679 are characterized by a median time to maximum
concentration (t
max
) of 2.5 and 4 h, respectively, and the ter-
minal half-life (t
1/2
) ranged from 0.8 to 2.5 h for selexipag and
6.2–13.5 h for ACT-333679. The pharmacokinetics (PK) of

Although several studies have been performed to investi-
gate the PK of selexipag in healthy subjects [7–9], in PAH
patients [1, 4, 6, 10], with interacting drugs [1, 5], and in
specific populations [11], the effect of selexipag on CYP en-

induction of hepatic CYP isoenzymes by selexipag and/or
ACT-333679 is unlikely at clinically relevant concentrations.
Whereas CYP2B6 is primarily expressed in the liver [14],
CYP3A4 and to a smaller extent CYP2C9 are also expressed
in enterocytes [15, 16]. Potential induction of intestinal CYP
enzymes b y se lexipag was inves tigated in this study.
Midazolam, a short-acting benzodiazepine, is metabolized in
the liver and gut wall by CYP3A4 and CYP3A5 [17, 18]to
form the major active metabolite 1-hydroxymidazolam, which
undergoes further glucuronidation and is mainly excreted in
urine [19]. Midazolam is commonly [20]usedindrug–drug
interaction (DDI) studies since its PK are well characterized
[21, 22].
Methods
Subjects
The French Ethics Commi ttee (Comi té de Protection des
Personnes, Sud-Est IV, Lyon, France) reviewed and approved
the study protocol and any material provided to the study
participants. This study was conducted in full compliance with
the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. Twenty healthy
male subjects were recruited in this study. Informed consent
was obtained from all individual participants included in the
study. All enrolled subjects in this study were healthy male
subjects with a median (min-max) age of 27 (20–44) years and
a mean (SD) body mass index of 23.7 (2.8) kg/m
2

Study design
This study was conducted according to an open-label, ran-
domized, two-way crossover design. Subjects alternately re-
ceived a single oral dose of 7.5 mg midazolam alone (treat-
ment A) and on top of steady-state selexipag (treatment B). To
reach the maximum therapeutic dose, an up-titration scheme
from 400 to 1600 μg administered b.i.d. was performed from
day 1 to day 11 (day 1 to day 3 400 μg, day 4 to day 6 600 μg,
day 7 to day 9 1000 μg, and day 10 to day 11 1600 μg).
Midazolam was administered in the morning of day 12, 1 h
following the last dose of 1600 μgselexipag.
Based on the within-group standard deviations (SDs) [24],
with a sample size of 16 evaluable subjects and assuming a
ratio of 1.00, it was estimated that the lower and upper bounds
of the 90% confidence intervals (CI) for the geometric mean

Analysis of plasma concentrations of selexipag
and midazolam
Blood samples for the analysis of selexipag and midazolam
were collected in lithium–heparin tubes and tubes containing
K3–ethylene diamine tetra acetic acid (EDTA), respectively.
Trough concentrations of selexipag and ACT-333679 in
plasma were measured on day 4, day 7, day 10, and day 12
using a validated liquid chromatography method with tandem
mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) with some modifications
from what was previously described [7]. Deuterated selexipag
and ACT-333679 were used as internal standards. The lower
limit of quantification (LLOQ) was 0.01 ng/mL for both
analytes. The chromatographic system consisted of an analyt-
ical column (Kinetex® C18, 5 0 × 3.0 mm ID, 2.6 μm;
Phenomenex, Brechbühler, Schlieren, Switzerland). Mobile
phases consisted of water containing 0.1% formic acid (v/v)
and acetonitrile containing 0.1% formic acid (v/v). Mass spec-
trometric detection was performed with a triple quadrupole
mass spectrometer (API 5000; AB Sciex, Zug, Switzerland)
operating in electrospray ionization positive-ion mode.
Samples were quantified using peak area ratios. The method
was linear in the concentration range 0.01–20.0 ng/mL for
both selexipag and ACT-333679. Precision (% CV) of the
assay was ≤5.4% for selexipag and ≤7.2% for ACT-333679.
The inter-assay accuracy ranged from − 1.9 to 0.8% for
selexipag and from −5.9 to 2.6% for ACT-333679.

hydroxymidazolam were assessed during 24 h after adminis-

Safety results
Following single-dose administration of 7.5 mg midazolam
(treatments A and B2 [i.e., midazolam on top of steady-state
selexipag]), all subjects reported at least one AE, with a total
of 39 AEs. Out of these 39 AEs, 37 were somnolence and 2
were headache. All AEs were of mild (35) or moderate (4)
intensity (Table 2).
Following treatment with selexipag (treatments B1 and
B2), a total of 69 AEs were reported by 19 subjects (out of
20 subjects who received at least one dose of selexipag). The
most frequent AEs (i.e., >10% of the reported AEs) were
headache, jaw pain, myalgia, vomiting, and pollakiuria
(Table 2).
No SAEs or other significant AEs occurred during this
study. One subject prematurely discontinued the study due
to occurrence of AEs (moderate headache, moderate nausea,
and mild vomiting) on day 9 after the morning dose of
1000 μg selexipag. All AEs resolved without sequelae. No
clinically relevant mean changes from baseline in clinical lab-
oratory variables, vital signs, or ECG parameters were
observed.
Discussion
Selexipag is metabolized via carboxylesterases into ACT-
333679, a metabolite active at human IP receptors. CYP3A4
might play a role in the metabolism of selexipag but not of
ACT-333679 as shown in a previous clinical DDI study with
lopinavir/ritonavir [5]. It has been observed in vitro that
selexipag and ACT-333679 are inducers of CYP 3A4 and
CYP2C9 [1, 4]. These two isoenzymes are expressed at intes-
tinal and hepatic levels [26]. It is generally assumed that the
CYP3A family, including CYP3A4 and CYP 3A5, are the
most expressed CYP enzymes in and outside the liver, and
their contribution in drug metabolism is crucial [27]. The role
of CYP2C, and in particular CYP2C9, is of importance in the
metabolism of several drugs [28]. The mechanism of induc-
tion of CYP2C9 and CYP3A4 is similar and involves the
pregnane X receptor (PXR) [29].
Based on in vitro studies, selexipag and ACT-333679 in-
duced CYP isoenzyme mRNAs with free EC

and 90 nM, respectively, for CYP3A4 and 6 and 29 nM, re-
spectively, for CYP2C9. Based on the high protein binding

tration might lead to induction of CYP enzymes.
In the present study, a novel up-titration scheme was ap-
plied to reach the dose of 1600 μg. In previous clinical trials
[3, 7, 30, 31], following a starting dose of 200 to 400 μg,
increments of 200 μg b.i.d. were applied to reach the maxi-
mum dose of 1600 μg b.i.d. Based on the PK pr ofi le of
selexipag, the time frame of at least 3 days before increasing
the dose is sufficient to reach steady-state conditions. The up-
titration regimen used in this study allowed to reach more
rapidly the dose of 1600 μg compared to previous studies
and did not have any impact on the PK of selexipag and
ACT-333679. Trough plasma concentrations of selexipag
and ACT-333679 after 3 days of 400, 600, 1000, and
1600 μg selexipag b.i.d. fully matched previous data obtained
with the same doses but additional up-titration steps [7]. The
level of CYP induction obtained in this study by selexipag is
expected to be that in clinical practice as shown with
rifampicin, another CYP3A4 inducer, for which a maximum
induction effect is achieved after a 5-day treatment period
[32].

In conclusion, selexipag and its active metabolite, ACT-
333679, did not alter the exposure to midazolam and 1-hy-
droxymidazolam. In addition, the safety and tolerability pro-
file of midazolam and 1-hydroxymidazolam were not affected
by treatment with selexipag. Since midazolam is exclusively
metabolized by CYP3A4 and CYP3A5 and is considered a
reference substance to study interactions with these isoen-
zymes, a potential effect of selexipag on the exposure to other
substrates of CYP3A4 is unlikely. Based on the sample size
and the specific population enrolled in this study (healthy
young male subjects), conclusions on the clinical significance
are to be drawn with caution. However, it is unlikely that
concomitant administration of selexipag necessitates dose ad-
justment of CYP3A4 substrates.
